TANNEN'S CONVERSATIONAL STYLE FEATURES
Source: Deborah Tannen, "Conversational Style: Analyzing Talk Among Friends"
        New Edition, Oxford University Press, 2005

Note on page numbers: PDF page = printed book page + 21.
All quotes cite PDF page numbers (with book page in parentheses).


================================================================================
A. THE TWO OVERARCHING STYLISTIC STRATEGIES
================================================================================

1. HIGH-INVOLVEMENT STYLE (signals rapport / interpersonal connection)
   "All of these features can be understood as expressing (in John Gumperz's
   terms, putting the signaling load on) involvement, or rapport. Hence, I
   shall refer to the speakers who use them as exhibiting a high-involvement
   style."
   — PDF p. 61–62 (book pp. 40–41)

2. HIGH-CONSIDERATENESS STYLE (signals the need not to impose)
   "In contrast, the other three speakers expected and used strategies that
   expressed (or put the signaling load on) the need not to impose. Hence,
   I shall refer to these speakers as using a high-considerateness style."
   — PDF p. 62 (book p. 41)

   Both descend from Lakoff's three Rules of Rapport — Don't impose
   (distance), Give options (deference), Be friendly (camaraderie) —
   quoted at PDF p. 37 (book p. 17).

B. CANONICAL SUMMARY OF DIMENSIONS (Chapter 7)
================================================================================

"Following is a summary of the dimensions that have been examined:
 1. Relative personal focus of topic
 2. Paralinguistic features (absolute use and use of marked shifts)
    a. loudness
    b. pitch
    c. pauses
    d. voice quality and tone
 3. Expectation that enthusiasm be overtly demonstrated, for example, through
    a. quickness of response
    b. paralinguistic features
    c. free offer of related material
    d. use of questions (information, echo, etc.)
 4. Use of questions, including
    a. echo questions as back-channel
    b. information questions
 5. Pacing
    a. cooperative versus obstructive overlap
    b. timing of contribution, relative to previous contribution
    c. rate of speech
    d. floor-getting devices (increased amplitude, repetition of words)
 6. Use of repetition, for example
    a. to finish another's statement or add to another's line of argument
    b. to incorporate another's offer into original statement or argument
 7. Topic cohesion (and tolerance for diffuse topics)
 8. Tolerance for noise versus silence
 9. Laughter (when, how much)"
— PDF p. 202–203 (book pp. 181–182)

"These and other characteristic ways of saying things are used in the
production of specific conversational devices, for example:
 1. Machine-gun questions
 2. Mutual revelation/personal statements
 3. Use of ethnically marked or otherwise in-group–associated expressions
 4. Story rounds
 5. Ironic or humorous routines"
— PDF p. 203 (book p. 182)

C. "FEATURES OF HIGH-INVOLVEMENT STYLE" CHECKLIST (Chapter 2)
================================================================================

"1. Topic
    a. Prefer personal topics
    b. Shift topics abruptly
    c. Introduce topics without hesitation
    d. Persist (if a new topic is not immediately picked up, reintroduce it,
       repeatedly if necessary)
 2. Pacing
    a. Faster rate of speech
    b. Faster turn taking
    c. Avoiding interturn pauses (silence shows lack of rapport)
    d. Cooperative overlap
    e. Participatory listenership
 3. Narrative strategies
    a. Tell more stories
    b. Tell stories in rounds
    c. Prefer internal evaluation (i.e., the point of a story is dramatized
       rather than lexicalized)
 4. Expressive paralinguistics
    a. Expressive phonology
    b. Marked pitch and amplitude shifts
    c. Marked voice quality
    d. Strategic within-turn pauses"
— PDF p. 61–62 (book pp. 40–41)

D. CHAPTER 4 — LINGUISTIC DEVICES
================================================================================

1. PERSONAL VS. IMPERSONAL TOPICS (preference for the personal)
   "Thus I began to see one of the major differences in Chad's and my
   strategies: expectations about appropriate topics of talk between new
   acquaintances."
   — PDF p. 95 (book p. 74)

   "The assumptions that it is appropriate to talk about personal matters
   with new acquaintances and that people like to talk about themselves
   seem like givens to some speakers (e.g., me), whereas for others they
   are equally obviously not true."
   — PDF p. 102–103 (book pp. 81–82)

2. THE ENTHUSIASM CONSTRAINT (overlap-as-enthusiasm)
   "I was trying to encourage Chad to tell me, not by waiting for him to
   talk, but by showing him my own excitement and exuberance. The message
   is in the pace that I am creating: 'See how excited and interested I am?
   I can hardly contain myself.' I expected Chad to become equally excited
   and shout me down."
   — PDF p. 98 (book p. 77)

   "The overlap-as-enthusiasm strategy is reminiscent of the enthusiasm
   constraint I discovered for Greek speakers (Tannen [1981a] 1994)."
   — PDF p. 98 (book p. 77)

3. THE MACHINE-GUN QUESTION
   "A number of linguistic features make these questions seem like
   machine-gun fire, including high pitch, reduced syntactic form, fast
   rate of speech, and directness of content. In addition, the effect of
   all these features is intensified by the pace with which the question
   is fired in conversation, that is, the time that is permitted to elapse
   before the question is posed."
   — PDF p. 112 (book p. 91)

4. OVERLAP AND PACE (cooperative overlap, latching, fast rate)
   "Thus, rapid rate of speech, overlap, and latching of utterances are
   devices by which some speakers show solidarity, enthusiasm, and interest
   in others' talk. The resulting fast pace greases the conversational
   wheels when speakers share expectations about the use of these devices."
   — PDF p. 119 (book p. 98)

   ("Latch" is borrowed from Schenkein 1978; defined at PDF p. 104,
   book p. 83.)

5. MUTUAL REVELATION / PERSONAL STATEMENTS
   "This is a device by which a personal statement is intended as a show
   of rapport. By this strategy, the speaker expects a statement of
   personal experience to elicit a similar statement from the other.
   Thematic cohesion is established by the metamessage: 'We are intimate;
   we both tell about ourselves; we are both interested in hearing about
   the other's experience.'"
   — PDF p. 122 (book p. 101)

6. BONDING THROUGH HIGH-INVOLVEMENT DEVICES
   "Peter responds positively to my sudden focus of attention on his
   personal life… rather than just answering my sudden question as Chad
   did with 'Yeah,' Peter volunteers added information that provides
   matter for further talk."
   — PDF p. 127 (book p. 106)

7. EXPRESSIVE PHONOLOGY AND INTONATION
   (incl. paralinguistic shifts, response cries, code-switching)
   "An aspect of Peter's and my styles is expressive use of phonology and
   other paralinguistic cues."
   — PDF p. 128 (book p. 107)

   "My choice of a Yiddish 'response cry' (Goffman 1981) functions in a
   number of ways to establish rapport."
   — PDF p. 129 (book p. 108)

   "The entire interchange thus exhibits marked pitch shifts and exaggerated
   stress, which give it a sense of expressiveness and empathy."
   — PDF p. 130 (book p. 109)

8. PERSISTENCE (re-introducing dropped topics; multi-try contributions)
   "Peter, Steve, and I operate on the assumption that those who want to
   say something will find the time to say it… one of the characteristics
   of our style is that Steve, Peter, and I persist when we have something
   to say… Both Peter and I persist with contributions for two, three, and
   four tries."
   — PDF p. 131 (book p. 110)

9. TOLERANCE FOR NOISE VS. SILENCE (and tolerance for diffuse topics)
   "A concomitant of the persistence device, and the assumption that one's
   thoughts are of interest, is a tolerance for such distraction and
   diffuse talk, which is related to the tolerance for overlap."
   — PDF p. 140 (book p. 119)

   "What speakers of this high-involvement style cannot tolerate is the
   alternative to this strategy: silence."
   — PDF p. 141 (book p. 120)


================================================================================
E. CHAPTER 5 — NARRATIVE STRATEGIES
================================================================================

10. STORY ROUNDS (clusters with thematic point-matching, no orientation)
    "I use the term story round to refer to a particular kind of story
    cluster, in which speakers exchange stories of personal experiences
    that illustrate similar points… they require little or no orientation
    section… The very juxtaposition of stories furnishes thematic cohesion."
    — PDF p. 147–148 (book pp. 126–127)

    "The story round device clearly belongs to the high-involvement
    strategy members of the group. Nineteen of the twenty-one stories told
    in rounds were told by Steve, Peter, and me."
    — PDF p. 148 (book p. 127)

11. EXPRESSIVE VS. UNDERSTATED EVALUATION
    (internal vs. external evaluation in Labov's sense)
    "Steve uses marked intonation and voice quality to show disgust; Chad
    uses extra words and monotonous tone. Chad's is a style of nuance and
    understatement, Steve's of expressiveness and overstatement."
    — PDF p. 158 (book p. 137)

    "(Evaluation in this sense, following Labov [1972], refers to speakers'
    ways of showing their attitudes toward what they are saying.)"
    — PDF p. 158 (book p. 137)

12. GETTING TO THE POINT (lexicalized vs. dramatized point)
    "It is part of Steve's style to tell a story the point of which is in
    the intonation rather than the verbal content."
    — PDF p. 167 (book p. 146)

    "He never states that point; it resides in the intonation pattern and
    the juxtaposition of the stories."
    — PDF p. 168 (book p. 147)

13. MEANING IN INTONATION
    (constructed dialogue framed by intonation/voice quality)
    "It is interesting to note that in telling this story, Peter uses
    intonation and tone to frame direct quotations. He does not introduce
    reported speech by saying, 'He said' or 'She said.' Rather, he mimics
    his son's and his girlfriend's speech."
    — PDF p. 172 (book p. 151)

14. COOPERATIVE VS. IMPATIENT PROMPTING
    "I wondered about the difference between prompting someone in a
    cooperative way and dragging a story out of them."
    — PDF p. 170 (book p. 149)

    "Thus the question serves both as a prompt and a show of interest in
    the story as it has proceeded thus far."
    — PDF p. 171 (book p. 150)

15. WHAT THE POINT CAN BE
    (cultural assumptions about acceptable narrative points)
    "In addition to differences in expectations about what stories can be
    about, there are clearly differences operating about how stories are
    told."
    — PDF p. 183 (book p. 162)

    "Sally's strategy is not to impose, not to insult her audience by
    hitting them over the head with the point. But her style differs from
    Steve's in that intonation and direct quotation are not used to
    dramatize the point either. Sally's style combines the lexical
    understatement of Steve's style with the paralinguistic understatement
    of Chad's style."
    — PDF p. 183 (book p. 162)

F. CHAPTER 6 — IRONY AND JOKING
================================================================================

"One of the most distinctive aspects of any person's style is the use of
humor. Through intonation, pace, voice quality, and nonverbal signals, a
speaker can frame an utterance or string of utterances as 'not meant
literally.'"
— PDF p. 184 (book p. 163)

Cutler (1974) intonational cues of irony Tannen quotes:
 "1. nasalization of all or part of an utterance
  2. slower rate in all or part
  3. exaggerated stress on one or more parts"
— PDF p. 185 (book p. 164)

Tannen catalogues five distinguishable humor styles in the group:

16. MOCKING / DRAMATIZED IRONY (Steve)
    Exaggerating one's own or another's speech patterns; "dramatic irony"
    in Booth/Roy's sense.
    "Steve's most characteristic form of humor is a mocking style by which
    he exaggerates either his own or someone else's speech patterns. In
    this sense, his is an irony aimed at style."
    — PDF p. 187 (book p. 166)

17. BUILDING-ON-OTHERS' HUMOR (Deborah)
    "Frequently throughout the conversation I make jokes by changing
    slightly or adding to something that someone else has said."
    — PDF p. 191 (book p. 170)

    "In this as in previous examples, my characteristic style is to build
    on the humor of another speaker's prior turn."
    — PDF p. 193 (book p. 172)

18. DEADPAN / MOCK-SERIOUS IRONY (Peter)
    "Whereas Steve's irony is mock annoyed, mock tough, or mock solicitous
    and dramatized through exaggerated enunciation, Peter's is mock
    serious."
    — PDF p. 193 (book p. 172)

    "Peter never laughs after his one-liners, and he often follows them up
    with genuinely serious statements."
    — PDF p. 193 (book p. 172)

19. CAMPING / STYLIZED ACCENT (David)
    "David's humor is similar to Steve's in that he often speaks with an
    exaggerated speech style. Whereas Steve exaggerates Jewish speech
    patterns and his own fussiness, David exaggerates stereotypical gay
    speech patterns; in other words, he camps."
    — PDF p. 197 (book p. 176)

20. CONTENT IRONY / OUTWARD-AIMED IRONY (Sally)
    "Notice too that while Steve's irony is aimed at mocking his own style,
    Sally's is content irony; it is aimed outward."
    — PDF p. 199 (book p. 178)

G. THE SUMMARY CONTINUUM
================================================================================

"Conversational style, then, may be seen as a mix of linguistic devices
made up of features and used according to strategies for serving the human
needs for interpersonal involvement and independence."
— PDF p. 206 (book p. 185)

Speakers are placed on overlapping continua (subtle↔gross humor,
slow↔fast pace, low↔high % narrative, few↔many narratives), which
superimpose into a master Considerateness ↔ Involvement continuum at
PDF p. 207 (book p. 186).
